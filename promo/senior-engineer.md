# Promotion Criteria: Software Engineer → Senior Software Engineer

## Overview
This document outlines the expectations for promotion from Software Engineer (L4) to Senior Software Engineer (L5). Senior engineers own meaningful technical work end-to-end, operate with minimal supervision, and make the people around them more effective.

The bar is not seniority by tenure — it is demonstrated capability. Evidence should show a consistent pattern over 6–12 months, not a single exceptional project.

---

## Dimension 1: Technical Ownership

**Expectation:** The candidate takes full ownership of features and systems — from design through delivery and ongoing reliability — without requiring regular guidance.

- Scopes and designs moderately complex features independently; asks the right questions upfront rather than discovering problems mid-execution
- Writes code that others can read, extend, and maintain; demonstrates judgment about when to simplify vs. when complexity is warranted
- Owns the operational health of what they ship: monitors, investigates failures, and fixes root causes rather than symptoms
- Navigates unfamiliar areas of the codebase without needing to be walked through it

**Evidence to provide:**
- 2–3 features or systems the candidate owned from inception to production, including what was uncertain at the start and how they resolved it
- An example of debugging or fixing a production issue they weren't originally responsible for

---

## Dimension 2: Technical Judgment

**Expectation:** The candidate makes sound engineering decisions under real-world constraints. They know when to be pragmatic and when to push for the right solution.

- Identifies trade-offs (performance vs. simplicity, speed vs. correctness) and makes explicit, defensible choices
- Recognizes when existing patterns should be followed vs. when a different approach is warranted
- Raises concerns about technical risk or scope creep early — doesn't silently carry problems
- Has opinions grounded in evidence, not just preference

**Evidence to provide:**
- A decision the candidate made that involved real trade-offs — what were the options, what did they choose, and why?
- A time they pushed back on an approach (from a peer, manager, or product) and what happened

---

## Dimension 3: Execution & Reliability

**Expectation:** The candidate delivers. Projects they own land on time with acceptable quality, or they surface blockers early enough for the team to adapt.

- Breaks down large work into deliverable increments without losing the big picture
- Communicates status proactively — doesn't go quiet when things get hard
- Produces consistent output across a sustained period, not just in crunch mode
- Has shipped meaningful work to production with positive outcomes

**Evidence to provide:**
- A project that was harder than expected — how did they handle the difficulty?
- Evidence of consistent delivery over the review period (not just one big project)

---

## Dimension 4: Team Contribution

**Expectation:** The candidate makes the team around them better, even if they don't yet lead others formally.

- Gives useful, specific code review feedback — not just style nits
- Explains technical decisions clearly to teammates and stakeholders
- Is a resource for more junior engineers — answers questions, reviews work, unblocks people
- Participates in improving team practices (testing, docs, on-call processes) without being asked

**Evidence to provide:**
- Examples of helping a more junior engineer succeed on a specific task or problem
- A team-wide practice or process the candidate improved, however small

---

## Promo Packet Instructions

The promotion document should be structured as follows:

1. **Summary** — 2–3 sentences on why this candidate is ready for Senior. Written as if the manager is presenting to the promo committee.

2. **Technical Ownership Highlights** — 2–3 projects showing end-to-end ownership: what the candidate was responsible for, what was uncertain, and what the outcome was.

3. **Judgment & Decision-Making** — 1–2 examples showing the candidate made real trade-offs or pushed back productively.

4. **Execution Track Record** — Evidence of consistent delivery, including how the candidate handled a project that didn't go smoothly.

5. **Team Contribution** — How the candidate has made the team more effective, with specific examples.

---

*Leveling calibration reference: The question to ask is whether this engineer can be handed a well-scoped but non-trivial problem and trusted to deliver it — including surfacing blockers, making design decisions, and owning the result. They don't need to define the problem themselves yet; that's the Staff bar.*
