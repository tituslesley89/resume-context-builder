# Promotion Criteria: Senior Software Engineer → Engineering Manager

## Overview
This document outlines the expectations for transitioning from Senior Software Engineer (L5 IC) to Engineering Manager (L5/L6 EM). This is not a promotion in the traditional sense — it is a role change. The candidate is moving from an individual contributor track to a people leadership track.

The transition should not be driven by a desire to advance; it should be driven by genuine interest in and aptitude for the work of managing people. Technical depth remains important context, but it is no longer the primary job. The primary job is the growth and effectiveness of the team.

Evidence should demonstrate that the candidate is already doing significant portions of the EM role informally before the formal transition.

---

## Dimension 1: People Development

**Expectation:** The candidate actively develops other engineers — not just unblocks them, but helps them grow their skills, confidence, and career.

- Has a track record of growing more junior engineers: pairing, structured feedback, advocating for their visibility
- Gives feedback that is specific, actionable, and delivered in a way that lands well
- Has sponsored or nominated engineers for stretch opportunities (leading a project, presenting work, being on-call)
- Understands each person on the team as an individual, not just as a producer of output

**Evidence to provide:**
- 2–3 engineers the candidate has invested in, with specific examples of what they did and what changed for that engineer
- An example of giving difficult feedback — what the situation was and how it was handled

---

## Dimension 2: Team Execution & Health

**Expectation:** The candidate has demonstrated the ability to help a team deliver reliably and maintain a healthy working environment — even when things are hard.

- Has led or co-led projects involving multiple engineers: planning, coordination, unblocking, and delivery
- Identifies and resolves interpersonal friction or collaboration issues before they become serious problems
- Understands team morale and workload; knows when people are struggling before they explicitly say so
- Runs or meaningfully contributes to rituals (standups, retros, planning) in a way that makes them useful rather than rote

**Evidence to provide:**
- A multi-engineer project the candidate led: how was work divided, how were blockers handled, what was the outcome?
- An example of identifying and addressing a team health issue — could be friction between people, unclear ownership, or unsustainable pace

---

## Dimension 3: Technical Judgment & Delegation

**Expectation:** The candidate can make or guide sound technical decisions, and knows when to delegate vs. when to stay involved.

- Provides useful architectural input without taking over; creates space for others to do the design work
- Identifies when technical decisions are high-risk and escalates or gets the right people involved
- Has helped define technical direction for a team or project without writing all the code themselves
- Understands technical trade-offs well enough to represent them to non-engineers

**Evidence to provide:**
- An example of guiding a technical decision without making it themselves
- A situation where they helped a team navigate technical uncertainty or disagreement

---

## Dimension 4: Stakeholder Management & Communication

**Expectation:** The candidate communicates clearly with product, design, and leadership — manages expectations, surfaces trade-offs, and builds trust across functions.

- Represents the team's work and constraints to non-engineering stakeholders without over- or under-selling
- Pushes back on scope, timelines, or requirements when appropriate, and does so with data and alternatives
- Keeps stakeholders informed proactively — doesn't let surprises land at the last minute
- Has built working relationships with peers in product, design, or other engineering teams

**Evidence to provide:**
- A time the candidate had to manage a difficult stakeholder situation (scope creep, missed deadline, competing priorities)
- Evidence of building cross-functional trust

---

## Promo Packet Instructions

The promotion document should be structured as follows:

1. **Summary** — 2–3 sentences on why this candidate is ready to make the IC → EM transition. Should address both the "why management" question and the evidence of readiness.

2. **People Development Record** — Specific examples of engineers the candidate has grown, with outcomes. This is the core of the EM case.

3. **Team Leadership Experience** — Projects or situations where the candidate led a group of engineers, including how they handled difficulty.

4. **Technical Judgment & Delegation** — Evidence that the candidate can guide technical decisions and create space for others.

5. **Communication & Stakeholder Management** — How the candidate represents the team externally and manages cross-functional relationships.

6. **Why Now** — A short paragraph on why this transition makes sense at this point in the candidate's career, and what they bring to the EM role that is distinct.

---

*Leveling calibration reference: The key question is not "can this person write good code?" — they already demonstrated that as a Senior IC. The question is "does this person naturally gravitate toward the growth of others, and do they have the instincts to build and sustain a high-functioning team?" Be honest about the difference between someone who enjoys mentoring occasionally and someone who will find deep satisfaction in the work of management.*
