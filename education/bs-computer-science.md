# BS Computer Science — State University
Undergraduate | Sep 2015 – May 2019

## Getting into CS

I didn't come in as a CS major. I started as Electrical Engineering because it seemed like the "serious" engineering choice. Switched to CS halfway through sophomore year after taking an intro programming course as an elective and realizing I actually liked building software more than circuits.

The switch cost me a semester of credits, but I don't regret it. CS felt like the first time school was teaching me something I'd actually use.

## Courses That Actually Mattered

Most courses were forgettable in the way most courses are, but a few stuck:

- **Data Structures & Algorithms** — The course that sorted the people who could think about problems from the people who just memorized syntax. I struggled with it more than I expected. Ended up spending a lot of extra time on graph algorithms. Got a B+ and felt like I earned it.
- **Database Systems** — First time I understood why data modeling matters. Built a small library catalog system in PostgreSQL for the final project. The professor was obsessive about normalization and query plans, which felt excessive at the time and turned out to be useful constantly in real jobs.
- **Operating Systems** — Brutal but formative. Writing a toy shell in C and a basic memory allocator made everything about how computers actually work click. I stayed up three consecutive nights finishing the virtual memory project. Still think about paging when I'm debugging weird memory behavior.
- **Software Engineering** — Semester-long team project. Four of us built a web app for managing class schedules for a local community center. First time I had to deal with merge conflicts, vague requirements, and teammates who disappeared for a week before a deadline. More realistic preparation than I expected.
- **Machine Learning** — Took this senior year as an elective. Got a surface-level understanding of the core ideas — gradient descent, regularization, cross-validation. Enough to know what people are talking about, not enough to actually implement anything production-grade without a lot more study.

## Senior Capstone Project

Worked with two classmates on a web-based collaborative code editor (think a simplified Google Docs for code). We built it with Node.js, React, and WebSockets. This was 2018-2019, before I'd seen how badly real-time sync could go wrong at scale.

- Built the operational transforms logic from scratch for the sync layer, which was ambitious and probably a mistake — there are libraries for this
- Got it working for 2-3 simultaneous users reliably; fell apart with more than that
- Learned more from the things that broke than from the parts that worked

The capstone is what pushed me toward wanting to work on real systems with real users where the stakes were higher than a grade.

## Activities & Involvement

- **CS Club** — Joined as a freshman, ran it as president my senior year. Organized a few study groups before finals and one hackathon in spring 2018 that about 40 people showed up to.
- **ACM Chapter Member** — Mostly went to the monthly talks. Some were interesting, most were dry.
- **Hackathons** — Did three over four years. Won nothing but learned how to build something fast and ugly and demo it convincingly. That skill turned out to be surprisingly transferable.
- **Part-time work** — Worked as a campus IT help desk assistant for two years. Fixed a lot of printer problems. Not glamorous, but it paid for textbooks and gave me an early sense of what it's like to support systems other people depend on.
